Privacy Policy

Bookworm — a quiet Android book reader

Why in the hell would you read a privacy policy?

Honestly? Almost nobody does. They get scrolled past, agreed to under duress, and forgotten by the time the next screen loads. They are written by lawyers, for lawyers, in a language only lawyers actually want to read.

But — oh, right. You're a reader.

And readers, as it turns out, do read things. Even small things. Even slightly inconvenient things. Especially when those things are sitting inside the same quiet reader you opened to spend a little time with a book. So I figured: if you're the kind of person who reads, you deserve a privacy policy that's actually worth reading.

This is that policy. It lives inside the reader because the way you read matters here too. Each chapter explains one small thing about how the app handles your data — and, more importantly, what it deliberately does not do. There is no fine print, no legalese, and no clever phrasing meant to make you give up halfway through.

If you remember only one thing after all this, let it be this: the app is built to work offline first. Your books, your pages, your time with them — none of it ever needs to leave the device in your hand for the app to do its job.

Your library, your shelf

Imagine someone walked into your home, scanned every book on your shelf, took notes on which page you dog-eared, and then quietly slipped it all into a filing cabinet somewhere. Slightly creepy, right?

That is the opposite of what this app does. Your library lives entirely on your device — the books you have added, where you left off in each one, the time you have spent with them, and your reading preferences. All of it sits quietly in this app's private storage on your phone, and nowhere else.

There is no cloud sync, no online profile, and no server on my end that quietly receives any of it. I did not build that kind of infrastructure, and the app contains no code to talk to one.

The trade-off is simple, and honest: you stay in full control. If you uninstall the app, or clear its data from your system settings, your library and reading history go with it. I keep no backups and cannot restore anything once it is gone. Just like a real shelf — the books on it are only the ones you put there.

Confession: it does use the internet, briefly

Okay. I should be upfront about this.

There is exactly one thing this app uses the internet for, and it is not what you might be bracing yourself for. It is cover art.

A book without its cover is just a rectangle with a title on it. Less of a shelf, more of a spreadsheet. So when you add a new book to your library, the app quietly asks a few public book-cover catalogs whether they have a matching image, and if they do, it downloads one. That is the entire extent of the conversation.

These requests carry nothing about you. No identifier. No account. No device fingerprint. No record of what you read or when. Just the title (and sometimes the author) of the book that needs a picture.

Once a cover has been fetched, it is kept on your device so the same request never has to be made again. And if your phone is offline, or if you would simply rather the app stayed off the network entirely, the app keeps working completely normally. The only difference is that some books will show a plain placeholder instead of a cover. The reading is exactly the same.

What I don't collect (which is most of it)

This is the chapter where most privacy policies get suspiciously vague. This one is going to do the opposite.

The app does not collect analytics. It does not show advertising. It does not track you. It does not quietly call home in the background. None of these things are toggled off behind a setting somewhere — they are simply not in the app at all. Not in the build, not in the dependencies, not in any hidden corner.

As a direct result, the app does not know which books you open. It does not know how long you spend in them. It does not know what time of day you read, what device you are on, what country you are in, or how often you come back. It does not know who you are, because there is no account. It does not assign you an identifier, because there is no identifier to assign.

Put plainly: there is no pipe through which this kind of information could leave your device, because I never built the pipe in the first place. There is nothing for me to misuse, nothing to lose in a breach, and nothing for anyone else to come asking me for. The best way to protect data is to never collect it. That is the trick I am using.

Permissions: the short list

Android politely asks each app to declare what it needs. Most apps treat that as a wish list. I treat it as a checklist of things I would rather not have.

The app needs internet access for exactly one thing: fetching book cover images, the same one I confessed to two chapters ago. Not for analytics. Not for talking to any private server. Not for collecting anything.

It does not need a storage permission to open your books. When you pick a book, you do it through Android's own file picker, and the app receives only that one specific file. The same is true if you open a book from a file manager or share one in from another app — the app only ever sees what you have explicitly handed to it. Nothing else on your device is visible to it.

And here is the slightly longer list of what the app does not ask for: your contacts, your location, your microphone, your camera, your messages, your call logs, your accounts, or anything happening in the background on your phone. None of those are needed to read a book. So none of them are touched.

About the cover artwork

A book without its cover is just a rectangle. You already know this, because you are a reader, and you have probably stared at a Goodreads list of plain titles and felt how flat that is.

The cover images shown beside your books in this app are fetched from public book-cover catalogs, so that your library can feel like a shelf instead of a list. They are downloaded only when a new book is added, and only to be shown next to that specific book.

The app does not claim ownership of any cover artwork. All rights remain with the publishers, authors, and catalog providers the images come from. The artwork is used only inside your own library on your own device — never redistributed, never repackaged, and never placed in advertising. (Because, again: there is no advertising in this app at all.)

Why the app is the way it is

A short, honest aside — because the choices behind an app are part of how it treats you, and a privacy policy is a strange place to leave them out.

I love reading. I love building things. I love designing things. So I built this one for the readers out there — the people who want a quiet, dependable place to spend time with a book, and not much else. Right now it is just Android, just phones, and just the features you see in front of you. That is on purpose. A reader app should feel calm and finished in the small corner of life it occupies, even if that corner is small, rather than half-finished and stretched thin everywhere at once.

That same thinking is why there are no accounts, no cloud, and no analytics. Every one of those things would have meant another system to maintain, another place your data could sit waiting to be lost, and another reason to ask you for something I did not strictly need. Keeping the app local kept it honest — and kept it simple enough that I can actually stand behind every line of it.

Every feature in this app earned its place by serving the act of reading itself. The ones I quietly left out were left out for the same reason — not to take anything away, but to keep the whole thing pointed at one job. The hope is that what is left feels less like a tool with options, and more like a quiet room with a chair and a lamp.

If there is a feature you wish this app had — a format it does not yet open, a setting it does not yet offer, a small thing that would make your reading just a bit nicer — please tell me. Every request gets read, and the ones that fit the spirit of the app have a real chance of showing up in a future version. The best feature requests this app has ever had came from readers, not from me.

Getting in touch

You made it to the last chapter. Genuinely impressive — finishing a privacy policy from start to finish is the kind of thing only a reader would actually do.

If you have a question about this policy, a concern about how the app handles something, or simply a thought to share, the way to reach out is through the contact options on the app's Google Play Store page.

Because there are no accounts and no in-app messaging, the Play Store listing is the one clearly-named place where messages can come in. I read what is sent there, and try to respond honestly. If something in the app is doing the wrong thing, I want to know — that is genuinely how this gets better.

Any future changes to this policy will appear right here, the same chapter-by-chapter way you are reading it now. No surprise pop-ups. No consent dialogs designed to be dismissed. No fine print buried in a settings screen.
